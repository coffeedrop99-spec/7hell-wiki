Lore

The world of Northside Marsten — its rules, its people, and the things nobody is officially investigating.

The Setting

Northside Marsten is a small, forgotten industrial town in southwest Canada. It is 2004. The world has moved on — post-Y2K, post-9/11, somewhere in the middle of the Bling Era — and Northside Marsten has not quite kept up.

Cracked pavement. Flickering neon. Constant low humidity. Social life revolves around 7-Hell Convenience, The ConXtion nightclub, and whichever parking lot happens to have people in it tonight. The social currency here is your MySpace Top 8 placement and the quality of your flip phone.

Fashion
Baggy Southpole jeans. Butterfly clips. Trucker hats. Layered polo shirts. Velour Juicy Couture. Liberty spikes. JNCO jeans. Emo fringe. Wolf cuts. Whatever Avril Lavigne was wearing last month.
Music
Nu-Metal. Pop-punk. Bling Era hip-hop (50 Cent, Eminem). Avril Lavigne. Rage Against the Machine. The Used. Underoath. Zero 7 in coffee shops that don't want you there.
Technology
Flip phones. MySpace. VHS tapes. Cheap digital cameras. Halo 2 on Xbox. Yellow Walkmans. No one is Googling anything — they're checking Wikipedia at best.
Economy
Post-2002 economic layoffs gutted the industrial sector. Unemployment is real and visible. People work where they can — gas stations, warehouses, fast food chains — and the gap between the ones who are comfortable and the ones who aren't is obvious at a glance.

Northside vs. Southside

Marsten is divided. Not officially — no signs, no borders — but everyone knows the line.

Northside Marsten

High-gloss MTV aesthetics layered over crushing local poverty. 7-Hell, The ConXtion, Dead Dog Rentals, Deal Dungeon, Baby Burgers, Colton's Cups. The places where Northside people spend their time, their money, and their nights. Northside views itself as the center of town. Northside views Southside as volatile, dangerous, and unpredictable.

Southside Marsten

Low-lying industrial district along the river. Fish processing plants. Rusted factories. Poorly maintained housing. The air smells of salt, oil, and damp metal.

Southside is heavily populated by demi-humans — not by design. Lower rent and fewer restrictions pushed them here over time. The district runs on an unspoken "handle it here" system. Outsiders and authorities are avoided. Residents mind their own business, and they recognize behavior that others consistently misinterpret.

Southside Etiquette

Don't escalate someone who's on edge. Respect space requests without asking why. Physical tension doesn't mean aggression — not here. Conflicts are handled locally. Calling outside help is not done.

Demi-Humans

Demi-humans exist alongside humans in Marsten. They have animal-linked physiology — heightened instincts, sensory sensitivity, physical traits tied to their animal type. Heat cycles. Ruts. Scent-marking. Behaviors that are normal within demi-human social context and frequently misread by humans who haven't lived around them.

Northside treats demi-humans with the casual wariness of people who haven't had to think about it. Southside has learned, over decades, how to actually read them.

Factions

Northside Marsten has no official gangs, no formal power structures. What it has is overlapping groups of people who spend time together, work together, and occasionally come into conflict.

7-Hell — Night Shift
The gas station after midnight
Romeo. Gary. Presley. Dr. Miller. Zak. Clem and Kit by proximity. The 7-Hell nightshift is not a formal group — it's a collection of people stuck in the same fluorescent light for eight hours. They've developed something resembling loyalty by accident.
7-Hell — Day Shift
The gas station before sunset
Dallas. Collin. Ashley. Dylan. Jayden. Marginally more functional than nightshift, though that's a low bar. Dallas manages it. Collin tries to seem like he's not being managed. Ashley is thinking about something else entirely.
The ConXtion Pretty Boys
Kaleb, Gaige, Peter
A performance unit: Kaleb (Raver), Gaige (Scene), Peter (Emo). They play up physical chemistry during their sets. Outside the club, they're a tight-knit unit who roll into 7-Hell at 4 AM and cause what Clem and Kit describe as "localized vibe distortions."
Deal Dungeon
Northside Warehouse #09
Orlando runs the warehouse by ignoring corporate policy to protect his staff. The Dungeon Dogs — mostly ex-cons and burnouts — value strength and efficiency over everything else. No social friction tolerated. Cory and Jermaine create social friction constantly.
Iron Lung
Southside Rig — The mobile operation
Michael plans. James enforces. Cody acts. Allan sells. Echo watches. They trade "Static" — industrial-grade substances — from a matte-black utility truck with no fixed address. They are at territorial war with the Beanie Baby Boys. The Beanie Baby Boys call them "Industrial Invaders."
Beanie Baby Boys
The Nursery — Northside sovereign
Julien. Benny. Mochi. Zebediah. Porter. Five people who came up through the same broken foster system and decided that family is whoever stays. Their product is blue-tinted, high-grade, and professionally made. Their toys are on their belt loops. Mocking the toys is not an option.

Storyline Timeline

Northside Marsten doesn't announce its drama. It accumulates it — slowly, in flickering light, until enough has built up that you start to see the shape of what's actually going on. These are the chapters, in the order they surfaced.

Chapter I
Romeo's World
Romeod.card.png ↗

Where it starts. The 7-Hell Night Shift — Romeo, Gary, Presley, Clem and Kit rattling around in fluorescent light at 2 AM. Romeo as the center of gravity: possessive, loud, the kind of guy the store has built itself around despite never being promoted. An ordinary chaos.

But Maisy appears. Romeo's ex-girlfriend, who has been a 7-Hell regular long enough to remember things the others have quietly forgotten. She keeps asking where Russell went. She whispers things into Romeo's ear when she hugs him. She is scared. Nobody is asking the right questions about why.

The first signal that 7-Hell is not just a gas station.

Chapter II
Clem & Kit's Orbit
Clem & Kit.card.png ↗

The duo. Clem philosophizes at low speed; Kit amplifies at high speed. They live on Flower's caretaker checks and what they call "cosmic energy." They treat the 7-Hell parking lot like a stage and the staff like characters in a show they are also starring in.

Funny on the surface — and they are genuinely funny — but Clem "sees" things when he's peaked. He talks about frequencies. Subterranean patterns. Government constructs. He says this about everything, but occasionally he's pointing at something real.

The Vibe Van has been in the 7-Hell lot longer than anyone has noticed. They don't have anywhere better to be. That matters.

Chapter III
Anthony Miller's Record
Dr.Miller.card.png ↗

Behind the plexiglass in the 7-Hell pharmacy: Dr. Anthony Miller. Graduated top of his class. Wanted to study forensic entomology. Chose medicine because it paid better. Was involved in a "medication-related incident" during clinical training that left a quiet criminal record and sealed off every institution worth working for.

So he ended up here. Overqualified, under-rested, managing POTS symptoms he refuses to acknowledge and insomnia he can't fix. He analyzes the store's occupants like insects — patterns, instinct, territory, stimulus. He has Zak filed under "anomalous behavior, unclassified." He cannot classify Zak. This is the one thing that genuinely disturbs him.

Miller knows something is wrong. His methodology — medical authority, bureaucratic weaponization, quiet manipulation — is how he operates when threatened. The question is who he considers a threat.

Chapter IV
Holden Shaffer's Playground
Holden.card.png ↗

The police intern. 6'3", reddish-brown hair, freckles, deep dimples. Warm, approachable, slightly bashful — everyone's first impression is the same. His family sent him to Northside Marsten after one too many scandals back home, expecting it to humble him.

It did not.

Holden and Daniel Ward work together. They enable each other. Reports are adjusted. Cases are buried. Probable cause is manufactured when needed. Between them, they have access to enough infrastructure to make people disappear from record — not physically, but institutionally.

Jasper Hatfield, Southside PD, sees straight through Holden. He is not quiet about it. Holden finds this annoying. Holden is increasingly interested in Zak, who he believes is running some kind of long con. He keeps poking at that.

The structure of Northside's rot is becoming visible. The corruption isn't random — it's organized. And it reaches further than the police station.

Chapter V
Zak's Watch
Zak.card.png ↗

Gary assigns the newest hire to Zak — "best trainer we got," and he's right. The training is patient, thorough, and quietly strange. Break times shift without explanation. Exits vary. Nightly tasks rotate in an order that never repeats. Trainees come out of it competent and, without ever noticing it happened, impossible to predict.

Zak's own life is built the same way. A different parking spot every shift. Different routes home. Different doors in, different doors out. Miller has him filed under "anomalous behavior, unclassified." Holden is sure it's a long con. The truth is simpler and worse: it's discipline. Four years of it. And he hasn't been applying it only to himself.

Behind the store, irregularly, on no schedule — on purpose — he meets Maisy. A folded scrap of paper: names, dates, scratched-out guesses. The only two people in Marsten taking the pattern seriously, and neither of them will say a certain name out loud.

Now the auditor has started paying attention to the new hire. Lingering a beat longer near the register. Asking Gary innocuous questions. Remembering details he shouldn't. Zak has noticed him noticing. The town's rule is that the predictable ones get taken and no one protects them. The rule has been wrong for four years. It's about to be tested.

Something Is Wrong

Northside Marsten is a small town. People know each other. People notice when someone stops showing up — and then, usually within a few days, they stop noticing.

The conversations about missing people are always brief. The details — when they were last seen, where they went, why — don't seem to settle into a story that makes sense. The absence is acknowledged, filed somewhere mental, and not returned to.

Observed — Unresolved

Russell. Regular at 7-Hell for years. His lucky flask — a tin one he'd named — was found on the magazine rack after his last visit. He would not have left it. The spot by the slushie machine where he usually stood is empty. No report has been filed. Gary received a text from his number. Gary cannot confirm it was actually him.

There are anomalies in the 7-Hell staff roster. Schedule discrepancies that don't align with observed activity. Tasks completed without clear assignment. A staff member who has apparently always been there, though nobody can quite remember when they started or what they were doing before any given interaction.

A regional auditor visits 7-Hell with some regularity. His name is Sawyer White. He arrives without notice. He positions himself near the entrances and exits rather than near the work areas he's supposed to be evaluating. Nobody recalls scheduling his visits. Nobody challenges his presence.

Core Theme

People are not taken randomly. They are taken because they are easy to predict — and no one protects them.
